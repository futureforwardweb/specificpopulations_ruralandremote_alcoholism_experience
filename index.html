A young bloke, a small outback town, and the pressure to fit in. This is about more than drinking.

Population: 918

Meekatharra, WA.
800km north of Perth.

Jake is 22. He works as a jackaroo on a cattle station 80km east of town — the kind of drive where the red dirt track narrows until it disappears, spinifex catches the last light on both sides, and your phone drops out somewhere around the mulga scrub near the old bore.

By the time he hits the bitumen back into town he's been up since four. His shoulders ache. The dust is in everything. And already, without having decided it, he knows he's going to the pub. He doesn't think of it as a choice. It's just what Friday is.

"Out here, the pub isn't just a pub. It's where you exist."

Saturday morning

He finds the messages at 9am.

The light is already hard. He can feel the tin roof getting hot. He doesn't check the time — he knows from the heat and the headache it's past nine. There's $80 missing from his wallet. He doesn't fully remember the drive home.

He lies there and the hollow feeling — he can't name it but it's familiar now — settles in for the day. He reaches for his phone. It's face-down beside the bed where he left it. He turns it over.

Four missed messages from Mum. 2:18 AM. 2:19. 2:20. 2:47.

He stared at the screen for a long time. She'd rung at 2am and he'd been asleep in the car somewhere. She'd been awake in Geraldton, three hours south, not knowing if he was okay.

This happened last week too. And the week before.

What happened last night

One beer turned into eight. He got home just before 2am. The loop is smooth and familiar and almost impossible to see from inside it. The boys will message again next Friday. And he'll go again.

What could have been different

Jake didn't have
the words.

When the boys messaged, there was only one answer in Jake's head. He didn't even question it. Going to the pub wasn't a decision — it was just what happened next. The alternative wasn't available to him. That's not weakness. That's not a character flaw. It's a skill he was never taught.

He thought about it later. What would he have even said? "Nah not tonight"? They'd think something was wrong. They'd take the piss. It was easier to just go.

But was it? He's lying here on a Saturday morning feeling hollow and broke, same as last week. So what was easier, exactly?

Skill One — Assertiveness
What it actually is

Assertiveness isn't aggression, and it's not confidence. It's the ability to say what you need — clearly, calmly, without excessive apology — and let the response be what it is. In rural WA, this skill is almost never taught. It gets read as weakness. It's the opposite.

Decide before the pressure hits

Plan it when you're calm: "I'll have two drinks" or "not tonight." Having a position means you're not making the call when the boys are already three in and the pressure is on.

"I" not "you"

"I'm not feeling it tonight" is very different from "you lot drink too much." One is about you. One starts a fight. Real mates will cop the first one.

You don't owe anyone an explanation

"Nah not tonight" is a full sentence. Short, direct, no apology. The more you explain, the more you invite pushback. Say it once, mean it, and let the silence land.

Offer the alternative

"Not the pub tonight but down for a drive tomorrow." You're not rejecting them — just the drinking. That distinction matters, and they'll feel it.

Jake can't go back and use this on last Friday. But it's Saturday evening. And the boys have just messaged again.

Thursday evening

The quiet is the loudest
it's ever been.

He gets home before the sun drops. Parks the ute. Sits on the tailgate. The station is still. No messages. Mum called earlier — he let it ring out. There's a specific kind of weight that builds when you've got nothing to do, nowhere to go, and you're not okay and you don't know how to say it.

The solitude that feels fine on Monday is different by Thursday. He's been up since four. His shoulders ache. And there's no gym, no counsellor, no footpath to walk. The pub is the only place that makes the noise quieter. Or at least, that's what he's told himself.

He's got the ute keys in his hand.

What does Jake do?

He'd heard someone mention box breathing once. Something first responders used. He'd laughed it off. But he was alone in a paddock, it was Thursday, and there was nothing to lose.

He came back different. Not fixed. Not even close. But the thing pressing on his chest — lighter. He called his mum back. Didn't say much. She talked about the dog. He listened. When he hung up, he made dinner instead of driving to town.

Skill Two — Stress Management
What Jake found out

Stress management isn't a gym routine or a meditation app. It's having something to reach for before alcohol becomes the only thing. Name the feeling out loud. Move your body. Change the scene. Have three alternatives written when you're calm — not when you're not. The land out there is free and it always works.

It doesn't just affect Jake

The ripple effect.

Heavy drinking in remote WA doesn't stay private. It moves through families and friendships — usually long before anyone has named it out loud.

The week after Thommo's message

Small things.
Every day.

Jake doesn't suddenly get better. He doesn't have a breakthrough. There's no single moment where everything shifts. But he starts doing one small thing differently each day. None of them look like much from the outside.

Skill Three — Resilience
What the week taught him

Resilience isn't bouncing back. It's building the things that give you somewhere to put the weight — before Friday arrives. A phone call on Monday. A walk on Thursday. Booking one appointment. These aren't big acts. They're the structure that makes the next choice possible.

A few weeks later

Jake made one
more call.

Not to the boys. Not to Thommo. He sat in the ute one Thursday night with the engine running and the number on his screen. He'd saved it three weeks earlier but hadn't dialled. He thought he didn't need it. He thought it was for people in worse shape than him.

Then he thought about his mum awake at 2am. He thought about Liam. He thought about how many Thursdays he'd told himself he was fine.

He made the call.
