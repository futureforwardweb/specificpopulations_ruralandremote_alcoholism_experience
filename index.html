A young bloke, a small outback town, and the pressure to fit in. This is about more than drinking.

Population: 918

Meekatharra, WA.
800km north of Perth.

Jake is 22. He works as a jackaroo on a cattle station 80km east of town — the kind of drive where the red dirt track narrows until it disappears, spinifex catches the last light on both sides, and your phone drops out somewhere around the mulga scrub near the old bore.

By the time he hits the bitumen back into town he's been up since four. His shoulders ache. The dust is in everything. And already, without having decided it, he knows he's going to the pub. He doesn't think of it as a choice. It's just what Friday is.

"Out here, the pub isn't just a pub. It's where you exist."

Saturday morning

He finds the messages at 9am.

The light is already hard. He can feel the tin roof getting hot. He doesn't check the time — he knows from the heat and the headache it's past nine. There's $80 missing from his wallet. He doesn't fully remember the drive home.

He lies there and the hollow feeling — he can't name it but it's familiar now — settles in for the day. He reaches for his phone. It's face-down beside the bed where he left it. He turns it over.

Four missed messages from Mum. 2:18 AM. 2:19. 2:20. 2:47.

She'd rung at 2am and he'd been asleep in the car somewhere. She'd been awake in Geraldton, three hours south, not knowing if he was okay. He stared at the screen for a long time.

This happened last week too. And the week before. And he still can't name the thing sitting on his chest — but he can't pretend it isn't there anymore either.

What happened last night

One beer turned into eight. He got home just before 2am. The loop is smooth and familiar and almost impossible to see from inside it. The boys will message again. And he'll go again.

Jake's been late twice this month. Macca — the station manager — has watched Friday absences bleeding into Mondays. He was raised in the same culture. He doesn't know how to start.

"He's a good kid" is as far as he's got.

All perspectives discovered

Before Jake even wakes up on Saturday morning, his mum has been awake for hours. His 16-year-old brother is showing his mates the posts. Thommo is wondering how to say something. His boss is keeping count. And none of them know how to start that conversation.

What could have been different

Jake didn't have
the words.

You just saw what last Friday cost. His mum awake at 2am. His brother reposting it like it was aspirational. Thommo typing a message and deleting it.

Jake knows none of this on Saturday morning. But you do. And the thing is — it could have gone differently. Not with willpower. Not with him being a different person. With one thing he was never taught.

He thought about it later. What would he have even said? "Nah not tonight"? They'd think something was wrong. They'd take the piss. It was easier to just go.

But was it? He's lying here on a Saturday morning feeling hollow and broke, same as last week. So what was easier, exactly?

Skill One — Assertiveness
What it actually is

Assertiveness isn't aggression, and it's not confidence. It's the ability to say what you need — clearly, calmly, without excessive apology — and let the response be what it is. Jake's clumsy message above shows exactly what happens without it: over-explaining creates the opening for pushback. Short and direct closes it.

Decide before the pressure hits

Plan it when you're calm: "I'll have two drinks" or "not tonight." Having a position means you're not making the call when the boys are already three in and the pressure is on.

"I" not "you" — and no novel

"I'm not feeling it tonight" is a full sentence. Jake's message was 40 words. Every extra word is an invitation. The more you explain, the more you invite pushback. Say it once, mean it, let the silence land.

Offer the alternative

"Not the pub tonight but down for a drive tomorrow." You're not rejecting them — just the drinking. Real mates will feel that distinction.

Jake can't go back and use this on last Friday. But it's Saturday evening. The boys are messaging again. This time, he knows the difference between the message that invites pushback — and the one that closes the door cleanly.

Thursday evening

The quiet is the loudest
it's ever been.

He parks the ute. Sits on the tailgate. The station is still. The solitude that feels fine on Monday is different by Thursday. He's been up since four. His shoulders ache. And there's no gym, no counsellor, no footpath to walk.

He called his mum back earlier in the week and it helped — briefly. But the weight is back. He thinks about what you saw on his phone: the messages she sent at 2am, Liam sharing his posts to 47 people. The thought sits in him differently now. It has a name. It has a face.

He's got the ute keys in his hand.

He'd heard someone mention box breathing once. Something first responders used. He'd laughed it off. But he was alone in a paddock, it was Thursday, and there was nothing to lose.

The difference tonight wasn't willpower. It was having somewhere else to put it before the pub became the only option.

Skill Two — Stress Management
What Jake found out — and what you saw on his phone

You saw who Liam is watching. You saw Mum awake at 2am. That context is exactly why this skill matters: stress doesn't disappear when you drink — it transfers. It moves to the people around you while you're temporarily not feeling it. Having something to reach for before alcohol — a walk, a call, a breath, a 10-minute drive into the paddock — isn't weakness. It's the only thing that actually reduces the load instead of shifting it.

Sunday night. After the message.

He put the phone down on the bench and stood there for a minute. He could hear the generator. The fly screen was rattling in the wind. Thommo knew. Of course Thommo knew.

He'd been carrying the thing that was sitting on him like it was invisible. Like everyone around him couldn't tell. Thommo just said it out loud and that changed something. Not fixed. Not even close. But it was like being in the dark for a long time and someone cracking a window — not much, but enough to tell you the sun still exists.

He went to bed early. He didn't drive to town. He didn't know yet whether he was going to do anything differently. But something had shifted — in the quiet, without anyone seeing it happen. He woke up Monday and made a decision. One small one. Then another one on Tuesday. Then another.

The week after Thommo's message

Small things.
Every day.

Jake doesn't suddenly get better. There's no breakthrough. But he starts doing one thing differently each day. None of them look like much from the outside. Each one just makes the next choice slightly more possible.

She picks up first ring. He doesn't say much. She talks about the dog knocking over the new kettle. He laughs — properly, not performed. The thing sitting on his chest since Friday is a fraction lighter. He notices that. He files it away.
Connection before crisis is the best kind. Not grand gestures. A ten-minute drive, a phone call.

The urge comes around 5:30, same as always. He sits on the step and watches the sky go orange. It's uncomfortable. He stays anyway. After twenty minutes, the feeling hasn't gone — but it's different. He makes dinner. Goes to sleep before ten.
You can't outrun a feeling. But if you don't numb it, it moves. Sitting with discomfort is a trainable skill.
WedBooks a telehealth appointment›
Three minutes to fill in the form. He almost closes the tab twice. He books it. He doesn't tell anyone. It's just for him. The appointment isn't for another week but already something has loosened. It's the act of deciding, not the appointment itself.
Asking for help is preparation, not weakness. Getting support before crisis is like checking your ute before a long drive, not after it breaks down.
ThuGoes for a walk before dark›
Twenty minutes out past the fence line. No phone. Just the red dirt and the spinifex and whatever is in his chest. He's sweating by the time he gets back. He notices his shoulders were up near his ears. He's been carrying them there for months without realising.
Physical movement processes stress that sitting still can't touch. No gym required. The land out there is free and always available.
Skill Three — Resilience — what this week is building

Resilience isn't bouncing back. It's building the things that give you somewhere to put the weight before Friday arrives. A phone call on Monday. A walk on Thursday. One booked appointment. These aren't big acts. They're the structure that makes the next choice possible — and makes it possible to pause for long enough to actually choose.

FriThe boys message. He reads it differently.›
5:31 PM. "Jakeyyyy you coming in tonight?" He's parked at the bore, still in his work shirt, eating an apple from the esky. He reads it. He doesn't feel the old jolt of obligation — the automatic yes. He actually pauses. He thinks about Monday morning and what he wants it to feel like. He puts the phone down and finishes the apple first.

That pause — that four-second gap between the message arriving and him responding — that's the whole week in one moment. That's what Monday, Tuesday, Wednesday and Thursday built. Not willpower. Structure.
The goal isn't to never go to the pub. It's to make the choice consciously, not by default. That pause — that's what a week of small things built.
A few weeks later

Jake made one

Not to the boys. Not to Thommo. He sat in the ute one Thursday night with the number on his screen. He'd saved it three weeks earlier but hadn't dialled. He thought he didn't need it. He thought it was for people in worse shape than him.

He made the call.
